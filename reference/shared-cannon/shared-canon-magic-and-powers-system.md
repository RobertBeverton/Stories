# Magic & Powers System
## Felix & Alex Story Universe

> Companion to *felix_alex_story_generation_guide.md*. The rulebook for how powers work — what arrives, when, to whom, and with what limits. Consistency here is what makes the powers feel like a real system rather than a new invention every time.

---

## How Powers Arrive

Powers arrive in **Mario-style question mark boxes**. On significant Saturdays, the boxes are gold.

**Golden rule: powers arrive *before* the action, always at the start of the story**, so they can be tested, misused, and creatively applied throughout — not just deployed once at the end like a key. Stories where powers arrived after the investigation had already begun consistently worked less well (the experimentation is the fun).

---

## The Core Rules

1. **Powers match personality.**
   - Felix → active, physical, loud (flying, magnetism, time-stop, invisibility, jumping, gravity control, warmth-power)
   - Alex → perceptive, quiet, precise (stillness, size-changing, truth-mirror, touch-history, calm-field, invisibility)
   - Amelia → analytical, strategic
   - When a power breaks this pattern (e.g. Felix gets something quiet, Alex gets something loud), **note it in the story** — it should read as a deliberate surprise, not an inconsistency.

2. **One power per person per story**, as a default.
   - Occasionally a second power arrives mid-story as a plot development — but a story should never *start* with two powers per person.

3. **Powers have limits.** Limits are where the tension and creativity live.
   - Time-stop → limited duration
   - Teleport → within 1km only
   - Baby-finger → only works on people already acting like babies
   - Every new power should come with at least one built-in limit before the story uses it.

4. **The biscuit rule.**
   - Robin once received a biscuit instead of a power, because he didn't need extra powers — he's already exceptional.
   - This is now canon and can recur for any character who's already established as exceptional (older allies, adults, senior guest characters).

5. **Unstable powers are a valid story device** — when the boxes themselves have shifting question marks, powers change every few uses. Creates comedy and stakes simultaneously. Used successfully in Case File #19 (the unstable powers problem).

6. **Powers can persist across stories.**
   - The "pointing" powers (baby finger, secret-revealing, brave-making) ran across two separate stories — a good model for rewarding continuity listeners.
   - Some powers become semi-permanent character traits rather than one-story tools (Felix's warmth-power stayed "always slightly on" after Case File #25 — he stopped noticing it, but everyone around him did).

## Villain-Side Magic

Villain schemes should have **one clear mechanism** a five-year-old can understand and explain back:
- A machine that sucks happiness (reverse the machine → happiness returns)
- A device that shrinks people (Gerald Pritt)
- A power-box gone unstable (Case File #19)

**Rule:** Every threat needs a visible, concrete, sensory manifestation. "Someone stole happiness" works because happiness has physical consequences (sad faces, a flattened world, silent birds). Abstract, non-visualisable threats don't work at this age.

---

## Objects vs. Powers

Not everything magical is a "power" — many stories centre on a **physical object** instead (the treasure map, the seed packet, the bark note, the compass that doesn't point north). Objects and powers can coexist in one story but the guide's advice is to always be able to answer: *"What is the physical object at the centre of this story?"* — even in a powers-driven case file, there's often a linked object (the perfume bottle device, the growing stone).

## Quick Pre-Story Checklist

Before generating a new story, decide:
- [ ] What power(s) arrive, and to whom?
- [ ] What's each power's built-in limit?
- [ ] Does the power match the character's established personality (or is the mismatch a deliberate surprise)?
- [ ] Do the powers arrive at the very start, before the investigation begins?
- [ ] Is there also a physical object at the centre of the story?
- [ ] Is the villain's mechanism concrete enough for a five-year-old to explain back afterwards?
