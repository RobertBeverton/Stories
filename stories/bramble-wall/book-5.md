# The Bramble Wall
## Book Five — More Under Than Over

Spring had crept up on the forest the way spring always does — not one big morning of it, but a hundred small ones, until Felix stood at the back door and realised the smell coming off the wet ground was green again, properly green, for the first time since the flood.

"It's going down," he said. "Alex. The water. I can feel it going down."

Alex was at the shelf, radio in hand, and didn't need telling. He'd been watching the garden puddles shrink all week, one dry inch at a time.

"Charged," he said, clicking his radio on. Green light, first try.

Felix clicked his. Green light, first try, and he held it up like a small trophy. "Charged," he said, "*and* I didn't even have to think about it," which was true, and which he seemed almost disappointed wasn't a bigger story than that.

"That's the idea," said Alex, and meant it kindly.

Out in the garden, the pool cover lay folded by the fence, waiting to go back up now the cold had properly broken — the same cover that had come down for winter, the same one that would go up again this week, the way it always did, the year turning all the way round.

Daddy came through in his enduro boots, helmet already under one arm. "Forest?" he said.

"Forest," said Felix. "You too — but not *with* us."

"That's the arrangement." He glanced at the sky, which was pale and bright and finally, after months, looked like it meant it. "I'll be out on the lower loop — the flood's mostly gone off it now, but there'll still be soft patches, so mind your footing. Radio if you need me."

"We always go together," said Felix.

"We always have someone on the radio," said Alex.

"Good lads. Go on, then."

---

Out the gate, up the footpath, and the trees closed over, and the sound went quiet-and-big, the way it always did — and today, under that, was something else: a steady, quiet *drip and trickle*, water finding its way down off leaves and out of hollows, the whole forest still faintly wet from months of sitting under itself.

The low ground that had been black flat water all winter was ground again — not dry, not yet, but ground, dark and soft and shining, with here and there a last silver puddle catching the sky. Green was coming up through it already, small and pale and determined.

"It's a hundred little forests again," Felix said, "with mud in between instead of water."

Alex crouched at the edge of the old flood-line, where the mud lay smoothed flat and glossy, like something had pressed it down and only just let go. He didn't say anything. He was looking the way he looked at everything — properly, all the way down.

---

They went the whole familiar route without needing to think about it — past the bramble wall, through the gap, Felix's hood catching on the same thorn it always caught on — and stood in the clearing, and for a moment neither of them said anything at all.

The den stood in the spring light exactly where it had always stood, against the fallen oak, and it did not look, any longer, like a lean-to that might blow over. It looked like a place. The door Elfine had woven still swung on its bark-strip hinge. The cleared approach still ran wide enough to wheel a bike through, though the bike-stand's two branches had a soft green fur of new moss climbing them. The dry corner Alex had built before the flood stood exactly as it had stood the night it kept them warm in the dark, packed turf a little more settled into itself now, a little more like it had always been there.

"It's not new anymore," Felix said, running a hand along the fern-thatch roof. "It's just... ours. Properly ours."

"It was always going to end up like this," said Alex. "You just build it a bit at a time and then one day you look and it's finished being new."

They went in, out of habit, and stood a moment in the small, held space of it — the flat treasure-shelf Alex had wedged inside back in the second summer, holding nothing much at the moment, waiting. Felix banged his head on the same low branch he banged it on every single time, said "ow," said "I'm fine," and ducked under it properly on the way back out, the way he always eventually did.

---

It was Alex who found it, the way Alex found most things — not looking for anything, just looking.

They'd gone down toward the old scorched patch near the hollow way, where the ground had always run black and bare and good for finding things in, back before the flood put it under water for months. The mud here had dried into the shape the water left it — smoothed, slumped, a little sunken where the flood had sat longest and hardest, right at the foot of an old alder that leaned out over the flood-line.

Alex stopped.

There was a shape in the bank, at the alder's roots, where months of standing water had worked patiently at the soil around it, softening it, sliding it away a little at a time, the way water does when it isn't in any hurry at all. Not deposited there. Just uncovered — the ground around it worn thinner and thinner all winter until what had been buried for years was simply, finally, showing.

"Felix." Alex didn't raise his voice. He never needed to. "Come and look at this."

Felix came, and crouched, and went very quiet, which for Felix was the loudest kind of interested.

It was metal — you could tell that much even caked in mud, by the dull not-quite-shine of it — and it was long, longer than Alex's forearm, narrower at one end than the other, one edge of it still holding something almost like a line, almost like a shape somebody had meant to make.

"It's not a stone," Felix breathed. "It's not a curl like Elfine's iron. It's — it's a *thing*. Somebody *made* this."

"It's been here the whole time," Alex said, quiet, mostly to himself. "Under the flood. Under the ground before that. We've probably walked right past it every single time."

"The water dug it up," said Felix. "Instead of us."

Alex reached out and touched it, testing, the way you'd test a soft patch of ground — press, wait, see. It didn't move. He pressed harder, working his fingers into the mud at its edge, and it still didn't move, wedged fast where a root had grown around one end of it sometime in the years since somebody had last touched it.

He tried again anyway, digging in with both hands, bracing his boots in the soft bank — and it held fast, and his fingers slipped, and he sat back hard in the mud, breathing, cross with it for one whole minute the way you're allowed to be cross with a thing that won't cooperate.

Felix didn't laugh, quite. He wanted to. He pressed his lips together and mostly managed not to.

Alex looked at the root again, properly this time, not just wanting it gone. "It's stuck," he said. "Properly stuck. Not a pull-it-out stuck. A dig-it-out-carefully stuck, and I don't want to just yank it and snap whatever it is."

Felix looked at it a long moment; then looked at Alex.

"We need Dad," he said.

It surprised him a little, hearing himself say it. Not *we need to show Dad* — that thought was in there too, somewhere, wanting to shout that the whole year had been real and here was the proof of it. But underneath that was something plainer: the root, the wedge, the careful digging it would take not to break whatever this was. A job for a bigger hand and a proper tool. Not a solver. Just help, the kind you ask for because the job actually needs it.

Alex was already reaching for his radio.

---

"Dad. It's Alex. Over."

A crackle, and then Daddy's voice, easy, a little muffled by his helmet. "Go ahead."

"We found something. In the ground, by the old scorched patch. It's stuck under a root and we don't want to break it getting it out. Can you come? Over."

A pause — the kind that had once, a long time ago, meant something was wrong, and now, this once, plainly didn't.

"On my way. Two minutes. Over and out."

They heard the enduro before they saw it, the buzz growing, then breaking off into the particular quiet of an engine switched off and walked the rest of the way. Daddy came through the trees on foot, helmet under his arm, and crouched down at the alder roots beside them without being asked twice.

He didn't touch it straight away. He looked first — the way Alex looked at things, Felix thought, and wondered if that was where Alex got it, or the other way round.

"Well," Daddy said. "That's not nothing."

He worked at the root with the little folding saw he carried on the bike for branch-clearing, easing it back rather than cutting straight through, freeing the packed earth in small patient scoops with his fingers the way Alex had been doing, only faster, only stronger. He didn't take over. He didn't lift it free himself and hand it to them finished. He loosened what needed loosening, and then he sat back on his heels.

"Your job now," he said. "Go steady."

Alex worked his fingers back in, and this time, with the root eased and the packed earth broken up, it gave — not with a pop, but a long, slow, sucking give, the ground letting go of something it had held for a very long time. He lifted it clear and laid it across both palms.

Mud-caked, pitted, one edge worn thin and the other still thick and solid — a blade, or what had been one, hand-forged a long, long time ago by somebody who was never going to know two boys and their father would be crouched over it in a spring wood a hundred years or more later.

Daddy turned it over once, ran a thumb along the worn edge to be sure it wouldn't cut, checked it over the way he checked everything they dug up. Then he sat back properly, on the wet ground, not minding it, and looked at the three of them — the blade, the boys, the whole quiet clearing with the flood-mud drying all around it.

"More under than above," he said. "Out here. Always has been."

He looked at Alex, then Felix, and didn't smile exactly, but something in his face did the same job as a smile.

"You found how to look. That's the whole thing, isn't it. Most people just walk on top of a place. You two actually look at it."

He didn't say anything else. He didn't ask what they'd do with it, or tell them where to put it, or turn it into a lesson with a beginning and an end. He just sat there a moment longer, in the boys' world, having been let into it once, and let that be enough.

---

They cleaned it as best they could at the fallen oak, working mud out of its pits with a stick and their thumbnails, and Felix held it up to the light, turning it, imagining out loud who might have made it and why and what they'd been trying to build with it, story after story, none of them needing to be true.

"We should see all of it," Felix said suddenly. "The whole year. From somewhere. All at once."

Alex looked at him, and then, without either of them quite deciding it together, they both looked at the same thing: the fallen oak itself, lying along the whole side of the clearing, its mossy back broad and long and higher, just now, than either of them had ever actually stood.

They'd sat on it. Hidden things beside it. Dug the black stone out of the ground next to it, an entire year ago. Neither of them had ever climbed up onto it.

Alex went first, finding a knot to push off from, hauling himself up onto the wide mossy spine of the trunk, and reached a hand down for Felix, who scrambled up beside him, both of them a little unsteady on the give of the moss.

And from up there — not high, really, just a few feet higher than a boy could reach on his own two legs — the whole clearing opened out at once.

The bramble wall, bare in patches from winter, greening back in already. The gap at its foot, worn smooth now by a year of two boys' knees. The cleared track line running off toward the hollow way, still faintly visible where new growth hadn't quite softened it yet. The den, tucked in below them at the very trunk they stood on, small and complete and entirely theirs. And beyond it all, the flood-line — a long pale smudge across the lower ground, marking exactly how far the water had come, and exactly how far it had gone again.

Nobody said what it meant. Felix just stood there, quiet for once, turning slowly on the spot, and Alex stood beside him, doing the same, and that was the whole thing, right there, without either of them needing to put it into words.

---

On the way back they stopped at their own cache, without discussing it, the way you stop somewhere your feet already know. Alex had to dig for it a moment — the moss had grown thick over the lid, thicker than either of them remembered — and Felix got mud flicked in his eye for his trouble and said "oi" and wiped it on his sleeve. Inside, exactly as they'd left them: Marion's holed stone, Elfine's curl of iron, Felix's glossy feather.

Felix held the feather up to the light a moment, the way he had the very day he'd found it, then put it carefully back.

"Still here," he said. "Still ours."

Alex pressed the moss back over the lid, so it looked, to anyone else, like nothing at all.

---

The gatepost oak was last, and they both knew it would be, the way you know the last stop on a walk before you've said so out loud.

The hollow sat where it always sat, dry even now, holding what it had always held: one acorn, three small stones in a row, a scrap of bark folded soft with age. Alex had looked at it every single time they'd passed, for a whole year, and never once touched it.

Felix took the two black stones out of his pocket — the heavy one from the very first day, and the smaller, glassier one Alex had found the summer the cousins came — and held them both, one in each hand, weighing them.

"We should leave one," he said. "For whoever comes after. The way somebody left this for us."

Alex didn't answer straight away. He looked at the two stones a long moment, and then he picked the smaller one out of Felix's palm — the one he'd found himself — and set it down in the hollow, next to the acorn, next to the three old stones, next to the soft folded bark.

He didn't say why he chose that one and not the other. He didn't need to. Some things you just knew the shape of, once you'd held them long enough.

Felix closed his hand around the one that was left — the first one, the heaviest, the one that had started all of it — and put it back in his pocket.

Neither of them said the word *goodbye,* because it wasn't that, not really. It was more like leaving a chair pulled out at a table, for somebody who hadn't sat down yet.

---

They walked home in the last of the soft spring light, mud-legged and quiet in the good way, the blade wrapped in Felix's coat, the flood-line falling away behind them, green pushing up through everything it had covered all winter.

At the windowsill, Felix set the kept black stone back in the very centre of the shelf, where it had sat the first night of all. Around it: the feather, the curl of iron, the small white bird's skull, and, last of all, laid carefully at the front where the light caught it, the blade. There was a gap beside the centre stone where the second one used to sit, and neither of them filled it.

The shelf was full. There wasn't really room left to add anything else, and looking at it, neither of them felt like there needed to be.

"Tell it," said Felix, sofa-flopped and thawing in the evening warmth, the pool cover finally off outside and the water starting to fill behind the glass door. "The whole year. All of it, start to finish."

So they told it, tumbling over each other the way they always did — the gap and the storm and the first den, the cousins and the stuck welly and the tin under the root, the ramp that wouldn't stand until it could be seen small first, the flood and the dark and the dry corner that held — and when they got to today, Felix told the blade and the climb and Dad crouched in the mud with them, and Alex told the hollow, quietly, in his own few words, and didn't say what it meant, and didn't need to.

Somewhere in the telling, the radio on the shelf gave a soft hiss, and under it, once, so faint it might have been nothing at all — *tock* — and neither boy went still, and neither boy stopped talking, and neither of them needed to say what it was, because they both already knew, the way you know a sound you've grown up with.

"Just the radio," said Daddy, from the kitchen, not even looking over.

"Yeah," said Alex, and meant something slightly different by it than Daddy did, and that was alright. Some things you could both be right about at once.

The four lights went red in a row on the charger, filling up for next time, the way they always did, the way they always would — in a forest a few minutes past the front gate, with a den in it that was finished being new, and a hollow with one stone more in it than it used to have, and two boys who had spent a whole year learning exactly how to look.

*The End.*
