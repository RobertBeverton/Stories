# The Troll Who Forgot How To Laugh

### *A Story for Felix and Alex*

---

They were walking back from the forest — a regular Tuesday walk, nothing special, just the three of them and the field and the path through the long grass — when Alex stopped dead.

Felix walked two steps further and turned around.

"What?"

Alex was looking at the hedgerow along the lane. Specifically at a section of it that was grey.

Not autumn-grey. Not frost-grey. The deep, flat, unhappy grey of something that had lost whatever made it itself.

One section. About the width of a doorway. The leaves still there, still the right shape, just — drained.

"That wasn't grey yesterday," said Alex.

Felix looked at it.

Amelia came to look at it.

"No," said Amelia. "It wasn't."

Felix reached out and touched one of the grey leaves.

It didn't crumble. It didn't feel wrong exactly. It just felt like a leaf that had forgotten why it was a leaf.

"It's spreading from something," said Alex. He crouched down. Along the base of the hedge, barely visible in the grass — a trail. Not footprints exactly. More like a path of grey, as if something had walked this way and the grey had come off it onto everything it passed.

Six toes.

Felix looked at Alex.

Alex looked at Felix.

Amelia was already writing in her notebook.

*What has happened to the Halloween Troll?*

The answer came back slowly, which was unusual. The notebook normally answered quickly. This one arrived like it was being careful.

*The troll laughed for a week after Halloween. Then the laugh ran out. Trolls aren't built for sustained happiness — it moves through them like weather, and when it passes, the grey comes back worse than before. He's been in his hollow in the mossy part of the countryside for three months. The grey has been spreading outward from him in a slow circle.*

*He doesn't know this. He isn't doing it on purpose. He's simply very sad, in the particular way of someone who was given a glimpse of something good and then watched it leave. The grey is grief. It just looks like the wrong colour.*

Amelia read it twice.

She showed it to Felix and Alex.

Felix read it.

He didn't say anything for a moment.

Then: "We should go."

"Yes," said Alex.

"Now."

"Yes."

Amelia looked at the grey trail leading away along the lane into the mossy countryside.

"It might be hard," she said. Not a warning. Just a fact.

"I know," said Felix. He looked at the grey leaf still between his fingers. "He laughed though. He properly laughed. That doesn't just disappear." He put the leaf down carefully. "It's still in there somewhere."

---

## The Boxes

They were on the five-bar gate at the edge of the mossy field.

Which was odd placing even by box standards — perched along the top bar in a row, slightly dewy, bobbing in a way that gate posts don't usually bob.

Felix hit his without breaking stride, jumping up to reach it.

*BONG.*

Warmth. Starting in his chest, spreading outward — his gold-power from Case #25, back again, the one that made things brave. But different this time. Fuller. Like the same instrument played by someone who had been practising.

He raised his hand toward the grey section of hedge.

The warmth-light touched it.

A single leaf — one — flickered back to green at the edge.

Just one. But real.

"Okay," said Felix. "That works. Slowly, but it works."

---

Alex hit his.

*BONG.*

He went very still — his stillness power, also returning, the deep clear-water stillness that let him know things.

He closed his eyes.

Opened them.

"He's close," said Alex. "Three fields over. In the hollow behind the big willow." He paused. "He knows we're coming."

"Is he—" Amelia started.

"Scared," said Alex. "Not of us. Of— himself. Of what he looks like now. Of us seeing it."

A pause.

"He's embarrassed," said Felix.

"Yes," said Alex. "Very."

Felix understood embarrassed. He understood it particularly well in the way that five-year-olds understand embarrassment — completely, physically, the full-body knowledge of it.

"Right," he said. "Then we don't make a big thing of it."

---

Amelia hit her box.

*BONG.*

Her power arrived as something she could only describe, writing it in her notebook later, as *reverse-reading.* She could look at something and understand its whole story — not just what it was now but how it had arrived at now. The path of it. Every hedge and gate and stone she looked at gave her a quiet history.

She looked at the grey trail.

She saw the troll walking it. Weeks ago at first — the grey just a shadow on things he touched. Then stronger. Then the circle spreading from his hollow outward as he sat and didn't move and the grey deepened.

She looked further along the trail.

She saw something else.

"The troll didn't lose the laugh," she said slowly.

Felix turned. "What?"

"The notebook said it ran out. Like weather." She looked at the trail. "But my power is showing me — something took it. Something came to the hollow about a month ago. Something small and quick and light-fingered." She looked up. "It didn't just fade. He was robbed."

Felix's eyes went wide.

Alex went still again.

"Who?" said Felix.

Amelia looked further along the trail with her story-reading eyes, following the thread of it.

"A Snip," she said.

"A what?" said Felix.

"A Snip. Small. Fast. Looks a bit like a pair of scissors if scissors could run." She looked up. "They collect laughter. Specifically recently-found laughter — the kind that belongs to someone who has never laughed before, or has laughed for the first time in a very long time. That kind is rare. That kind is valuable." She closed her notebook. "The troll's first laugh was exactly that kind. And a Snip came and took it while he was sleeping."

The field was quiet.

Felix looked at the grey trail.

"So he thinks the laugh left him," said Felix. "But it was stolen."

"Yes."

"And nobody told him."

"Nobody knew."

Felix stood up very straight.

"Right," he said. "Two things to do. Find the Snip. Then find the troll." He looked at Alex. "You track the Snip. Amelia reads the trail. I'll go to the troll."

"Alone?" said Amelia.

"Someone has to," said Felix simply.

Amelia looked at him.

Sometimes Felix was five years old and sometimes he was something older than that wearing a five-year-old, and the second thing was always slightly startling when it arrived.

"Right," she said. "Together as far as the willow. Then we split."

---

## The Snip

The Snip's trail was different from the troll's — quick and darting, here-then-there, the kind of path made by something that changed direction suddenly and often.

Alex tracked it with his stillness, standing at each point and knowing the next, until they found the Snip in the hollow of a fallen oak not far from the willow — a small, bright, metallic creature about the size of a large mouse, angular and quick, with two blades that weren't threatening exactly, more like very efficient-looking hands.

It was surrounded by small glowing things.

Laughs.

Dozens of them, each one slightly different — some big and round and golden, some thin and silver, some warm amber, some bright white. Stored in the hollow like a magpie's collection, each one with a faint sound that wasn't quite audible but which you could feel as a warmth at the back of the throat, the ghost of what they were.

The Snip saw them coming and went very still in the way of something deciding whether to run.

Alex sat down.

He sat on the ground in front of the hollow, cross-legged, no sudden movements, and looked at the Snip with his clear quiet attention.

The Snip looked back.

"We're not going to hurt you," said Alex.

The Snip made a sound — a quick metallic snick, like scissors opening and closing once.

"We need the troll's laugh back," said Alex. "The big golden one." He looked at the collection. "I think you know which one."

The Snip looked at its collection.

Alex waited.

The Snip moved toward a particular laugh — round, deep gold, still with the rumbling quality of boulders rolling downhill, the sound of a creature laughing for the first time in its whole life — and stopped.

"You kept it separate," said Alex. "From the others. You knew it was different."

The Snip made a smaller sound. Not snick. Something softer.

Amelia, behind Alex, was reading the Snip's story with her power, quietly, not interrupting.

"It's lonely," she said softly. "The laughs keep it company. It's not malicious. It just — collects warmth because it doesn't have its own."

Alex looked at the Snip.

"If you give the troll's laugh back," he said, "we'll come back and visit. Properly. That's a real offer."

The Snip looked at him for a long time.

Then it picked up the round golden laugh — delicately, with both blade-hands — and held it out.

Alex took it.

It felt exactly like what it was. Warm and round and rumbling and alive.

"Thank you," said Alex.

The Snip snicked once, quietly, and went back into its hollow.

Alex stood up and cupped the laugh in both hands.

"Go," said Amelia. "Felix needs us."

---

## The Troll

Felix had gone ahead alone.

The willow was enormous and old, its branches hanging down to the ground in long curtains, and behind those curtains a hollow — the troll's hollow, dark and mossy and quite large because the troll was quite large, with a smell of earth and damp and the deep nothing-smell of grey things.

Felix pushed through the willow branches.

The troll was there.

Much greyer than Felix remembered. Not just colour — shape. He seemed smaller, somehow, even though he was clearly the same size. Compressed. Folded in on himself, sitting against the back of the hollow with his six-toed feet in front of him and his large warty hands in his lap and his raisin eyes looking at exactly nothing.

He looked up when Felix came in.

And immediately looked away.

"Go away," said the troll.

His voice was different. The deep grumbly rumble was still there but it was flat. The way a bell sounds flat when you muffle it with your hand.

"No," said Felix pleasantly, and sat down on a root.

"I said—"

"I heard you," said Felix. "I'm sitting down anyway."

The troll said nothing.

Felix looked around the hollow. Grey moss. Grey roots. A small pile of things — pebbles, a stick, a piece of bark — that might have been sorted and arranged at some point and were now just there, untouched.

"I'm grey," said the troll. Not complainingly. Just stating it.

"I can see that," said Felix.

"It's spreading."

"I know."

"I'm not doing it on purpose."

"I know that too," said Felix.

The troll looked at his hands. Large, greenish-grey, warty. "The laugh," he said. Very quietly. "After Halloween. It was — for a week I kept — I couldn't stop—" He stopped. "And then it just went. One morning it wasn't there anymore and I thought at first it would come back but it didn't and—" He looked at his hands. "I think I'm just not something that gets to keep that."

Felix looked at him.

At this large grey sad creature who had danced his small smug dance on Halloween night and howled with laughter at his own happiness and eaten one toffee with enormous dignity.

"Can I tell you something?" said Felix.

The troll said nothing, which Felix took as yes.

"It didn't leave," said Felix. "It was taken. Something took it while you were asleep. Something called a Snip that collects first-laughs because they're rare and valuable." He watched the troll's face. "You didn't lose it. You're not someone who can't keep it. It was stolen."

The troll was very still.

"That's not—" he started.

"True?" said Felix. "My friend Alex has it right now. He's coming."

The troll looked at the curtain of willow branches.

Felix's warmth-power was spreading gently through the hollow — he wasn't pushing it, just letting it exist, the way you let a fire warm a room without pointing at anything specific. The grey at the edge of the hollow shifted slightly. Just at the edges.

"The grey," said the troll. "Did it—"

"The field hedge," said Felix. "A bit."

The troll closed his eyes. "I'm sorry."

"It's fixable," said Felix. "That's why we're here."

---

Alex came through the willow branches.

He stopped when he saw the troll. The troll opened his eyes and saw what Alex was holding.

The golden laugh sat in Alex's cupped hands, warm and round, catching no light because it made its own.

The troll looked at it.

His raisin eyes — which had been flat and dull — went different. Not happy yet. Something before happy. Something like recognition.

"That's—" he said.

"Yes," said Alex.

Alex walked across the hollow and held his hands out.

The troll looked at the laugh for a long time.

Then he reached out — carefully, more carefully than you'd expect from hands that size — and took it.

The moment it touched him the laugh didn't go back in gradually. It wasn't a slow process. It went back the way it had been taken — all at once, completely, every part of it, the whole first magnificent ridiculous joyful surprised laugh of the Halloween Troll's life, back where it belonged.

The hollow went gold for a moment.

Just a flash.

Then the troll laughed.

---

It was the same laugh.

Exactly the same. *HUH. HUH HUH. HEEHEEHEE. BWAHAHAHA.* The boulders-downhill laugh, the laugh that didn't know how to be small, filling the hollow and pushing out through the willow branches and sending three wood pigeons out of the tree above in a great startled clatter.

Felix started laughing too.

He couldn't help it.

Alex smiled — the quiet full smile he kept for the best moments.

Amelia appeared through the willow curtain, heard the laugh, and her face did the thing it rarely did, which was let everything she was feeling show at once.

The troll laughed until he ran out of breath, which took a while, and then breathed, and laughed again, and then sat back against the hollow wall and wiped his eyes.

The grey was going.

Not all at once, but unmistakably — the colour creeping back into the moss, the roots, the earth of the hollow. The troll's own greenish colour deepening, the flat drained quality lifting, his raisin eyes going bright.

"Oh," said the troll. He said it very simply. Like coming home.

"Better?" said Felix.

"Much," said the troll.

He looked at the three of them — Amelia at the entrance, Alex standing quietly, Felix on his root, the warmth-power still gently present.

"You came," said the troll.

"Obviously," said Felix.

"I thought—" the troll stopped. Then: "Nobody's come to the hollow before. In—" he thought about it— "a very long time."

"We'll come back," said Alex. "We told the Snip the same thing."

The troll looked at him. "You made a deal with the Snip?"

"It's lonely," said Alex. "Like you."

The troll was quiet.

"There's a support group," said Felix. Because of course he said it. He said it to everyone.

"You've mentioned this before," said the troll. "On Halloween. You said it then too."

"You didn't come," said Felix.

"No," said the troll.

"Thursday evenings," said Felix. "Victor makes a very good sponge now. Voldemort comes. He sits at the back. He ate two pieces last week."

The troll looked at Felix with his bright raisin eyes. "Voldemort."

"He's working on things," said Felix. "Like everyone."

The troll looked at his hands. Green again, properly green, the grey almost fully gone. He turned them over. Looked at his six toes.

"Thursday," he said.

"Thursday," said Felix.

---

## Going Home

The grey in the hedgerow was green by the time they walked back.

Not all of it immediately — it spread back the way the grey had spread, slowly, from the hollow outward, the colour following the laugh home. But the section they'd first noticed — the doorway-width of drained leaves on the lane — was already green and rustling and entirely itself by the time they passed it.

Felix touched one leaf as he walked past.

Just a leaf. Just green. Just a leaf being a leaf without any trouble.

He smiled.

"Good day," said Alex, beside him.

"Good day," agreed Felix.

Amelia was writing in her notebook — the Snip's hollow location, a note to revisit, thoughts on first-laughs and why they were rare and what it meant that the troll's had been so easy to steal (not because the troll was weak, she was writing, but because it had been so new, so unguarded, held the way you hold something you've just been given before you know it's yours to keep).

Felix looked over his shoulder once at the mossy countryside.

Far away, barely audible, carried on the wind like weather:

*HUH HUH HEEHEEHEE BWAHAHAHA.*

The troll, laughing to himself.

Just because.

Just because it was still there.

Felix turned back to the lane and kept walking.

Some things you just know are going to be okay.
