# Trio Force
## Case File #2 — The Fog Over Lipwood Lake

Daddy was standing at the kitchen window with his tea, looking out at the field, when he said the word.

"Fog."

"What fog?" said Felix, not looking up from the toaster.

"The kind that shouldn't be there," said Daddy, and took a sip.

Alex was already at the window. He was looking at the lake in the distance — the one it took forty-five minutes to walk all the way round, with the ducks and the old willow and the bench where Mummy sometimes sat and read. The lake they knew by heart.

Today the lake was different.

The fog sat on the water like a great grey blanket. It wasn't drifting. It wasn't lifting. It was just *there* — and something inside it was moving.

"You see it?" Alex said quietly.

"I see it," said Felix, and he was already up from the table.

---

At HQ, two gold boxes were waiting on the desks, shimmering with question marks.

Felix opened his first. Inside was a coil of red rope — not ordinary rope. Thrown, it flew *exactly* where you aimed it. It could catch a branch, a rock, a roof.

"Five throws," Felix read off the little tag tied to it. "Then it's just rope."

Alex opened his. Inside was a small blue compass. The needle didn't point north. It pointed toward *feelings* — swinging left for sadness, trembling for fear, spinning in slow circles for a lie.

"Three minutes at a time," Alex read. "Then it needs the same again, resting, before it'll work once more."

Felix looked at the rope. Alex looked at the compass.

"Whatever's in that fog," Felix said, "it's not going anywhere fast. So neither are we — carefully, this time."

Alex didn't answer straight away, which was how Felix knew he agreed.

---

The lake was worse up close. The ducks were gone. The old willow was just a grey shape, like a shadow of itself. The bench was wet with dew, though it hadn't rained all week.

Alex held up the compass. The needle swung hard to the left and stayed there.

"Someone's sad," he said. "Very sad."

"Is the whole fog sad?"

"Not the fog," said Alex. "Someone *in* the fog."

That's when they heard the voice. Not loud. Not frightening. A voice Felix recognised straight away, because he'd heard it once before, a long time ago, standing in a school hall looking at someone who had been, back then, very small and very cross.

"I knew you'd come," the voice said.

Out of the fog stepped Victor Spung.

Felix's stomach did something complicated. He liked Victor now — the new Victor, the Thursday-group Victor, the one who brought cake and had gone back to his proper size. If the fog meant Victor was slipping backwards, Felix wanted to know *today*, not find out in a week that the old, small, cross Victor was back for good.

---

Victor wasn't small anymore — he'd been back to his full size for about a year now, ever since he'd started going to the Thursday group. He wore the same cloak he always did, but he looked different. Older. More tired.

And soaking wet, like he'd been sitting by the lake since dawn.

"Hello, Victor," said Felix.

"Hello, boys," said Victor.

He looked at the lake, and sighed, and pointed.

"This is mine," he said. "Well — not mine. But *I* did this. A long time ago, before I started going to group. I buried something down there. Small. By that grey rock at the edge. For years it did nothing at all. But last night..."

He stopped.

"Last night it turned itself on," Alex finished.

Victor stared at him.

"How did you know that?"

"The compass," Alex said, holding up the little blue disc. "And your face."

Victor sat down on the wet grass, hard, like his legs had simply stopped being able to hold him up.

"It makes fog," he said. "Fog made of sadness. It gathers up sad feelings and turns them into... this." He nodded at the grey water. "I built it when I was unhappy myself. Wanted others to feel it too."

Nobody said anything for a moment.

"I'm sorry," Victor said, very quietly.

Felix looked at him for a long time.

"Why did it turn on now?" he asked.

Victor's hands were shaking a little. "Because last week I was sad again. Properly sad. I think the machine felt it. It's still... connected to me, somehow."

Alex checked the compass. The needle wasn't just swinging left anymore — it was trembling too. Fear, not just sadness. Victor wasn't afraid of the fog. He was afraid of what it meant about him — that he might be about to become the old Victor again, the small angry one, the one from before.

"Where is it?" Felix asked.

Victor pointed at the grey rock near the water's edge. "Down there. Two metres under. Right by the stone."

Felix was already looking at the rope in his hand.

---

The first throw went too far. It landed with a splash and sank, and Felix had to reel it back in, dripping.

The second throw fell short, curling uselessly on the mud.

"Two gone," Alex said. "Three left."

Felix took a breath, and looked properly this time — not at the water, but at the actual shape of the rock beneath it, the way it leaned, the way the light came off it. The third throw hit true. The rope caught round the stone and held.

Felix waded in up to his knees — cold, but nothing worse — and ducked under.

There, wedged against the rock, was a small silver box with a little wheel spinning slowly on top.

He grabbed it and pulled.

It didn't budge.

He came up gasping. "It's stuck fast to the rock. I can't get it free just pulling straight up."

Alex was already looking at the rope, and at the rock, and at the way the current moved. "Wrap it round twice. Pull from the bank, not from the water — you need your feet on something solid."

Felix looked at the rope. Two throws left. He didn't need another throw for this — the rope was already caught. He waded back out, wound the line twice around the exposed lip of the stone the way Alex said, and planted his feet on the bank.

"Ready?" said Felix.

"Ready," said Alex, grabbing the rope behind him. Victor, without a word, grabbed on behind Alex.

All three of them pulled.

*CRACK.*

The silver box broke free and shot up to the surface, bobbing.

---

Victor picked it up with shaking hands. It was small, and silver, and warm from the water it had spent so long buried under, and the little wheel on top was slowing — slowing — slowing —

And then it stopped.

The fog began to sink. Not fast — slow, gentle, like a blanket being lifted off a bed. The ducks came paddling back out from the reeds as if they'd never left. The willow found its colour again.

Felix wrung out his socks and looked at Victor.

"Is it Thursday this week?" he asked.

"It is," said Victor.

"Then go," said Felix. "And tell Janet you told us everything. That matters — telling someone *before* the machine turns on, not after."

Victor looked at him for a long moment.

"You're five," he said finally.

"Five and two months," said Felix.

Victor tucked the box into his coat pocket — then stopped, and took out the little wheel from its top, which had come loose in the pulling. He held it out to Felix.

"Keep this part," he said. "So you remember I gave the rest back myself. I didn't need you to take it from me."

Felix took it. It was cool now, and still, and about the size of a button.

Victor stood up, brushed off his knees, and — for the first time since they'd arrived, small and a bit shy about it — smiled.

"Right," he said. "I'm going."

---

They got home before lunch. Daddy was at the window with a fresh cup of tea.

"Fog's gone," he said.

"We sorted it," said Felix, peeling off his wet socks.

"I saw." Daddy took a sip. "Wet socks go in the wash."

"I know."

"And you did the right thing," Daddy added, in exactly the same voice he'd have used to talk about the weather.

Alex hung his jacket on the peg and checked his compass one more time. The needle sat still. Straight.

Nobody near them was sad. Nobody was afraid. Nobody was lying.

Not right now, anyway.

Felix put the little silver wheel on the shelf at HQ, next to the warm red scrap from the very first case. Two things now. He liked that it was becoming a two-things sort of shelf.

---

*Victor Spung arrived at the Thursday group at six o'clock exactly. Janet only said, "I knew you'd come back," and gave him a piece of shortbread — the best he'd ever eaten, though that might have been the day he'd had.*

*The rope's fifth and final throw happened the next morning, when Felix wanted to check it still worked. After that, it was just rope. He uses it for skipping now. It's very good for skipping.*

*On the walk home — before it finally settled straight — Alex's compass pointed gently right the whole way. When he asked what that meant, much later, and only once, all he got back was that right means lucky. He put the compass in his pocket and didn't say anything else. But he smiled.*
