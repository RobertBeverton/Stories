# Trio Force
## Case File #3 — The Day of a Hundred Felixes

It started, as things often did, with Felix having an idea.

"Alex," said Felix, at breakfast, with the particular look he got when the idea was *very* big. "What if there were more of me?"

Alex looked up from his toast. "More of you."

"Loads more of me. Like twenty me's. All doing stuff at the same time."

"Why twenty?"

Felix thought about it properly, for once. "Because then nobody could ever say there wasn't enough of me around. There'd just... always be a Felix, wherever you needed one."

Alex considered this for exactly three seconds. "That's either the best idea you've ever had," he said, "or the most dangerous."

"Both," said Felix happily, and ate his toast in one enormous bite.

---

They were on their way to HQ when Evy came running across the field, hair flying, eyes wide.

"Something's happening in town," she said. "Something *weird*."

"Weird good or weird bad?"

"Weird *duplicating*," said Evy. "Mrs. Okafor's post office has got three Mrs. Okafors in it. Mr. Pemberton pulled seventeen identical loaves out of the oven at once. The fountain keeper says there are four fountains now."

Alex was already watching the sky. Something in the air shimmered, faint, like the world had been photocopied slightly wrong.

"HQ," he said. "Now."

---

Two gold boxes were waiting on the desks. Gold meant today mattered.

Felix's opened with a sound like a trumpet played by someone who'd never met a trumpet before, and out came a small badge.

**MULTIPLIER — up to twenty copies of yourself. They share your bravery. They do not share your sensibleness. Use wisely.**

Felix's whole face went incandescent.

Alex's opened quietly, the way it always did, with a small chime like a single raindrop, and out came a silver whistle.

**RECALL — one blow brings every copy home. Two blows stops a multiplied villain. Three blows is for real emergencies only. You'll know when.**

"Twenty of me," Felix said. "Let's go."

"Wait," said Alex.

Felix had already pressed the button.

---

*BWAAAP.*

*BWAAAP BWAAAP BWAAAP BWAAAP BWAAAP —*

Twenty Felixes stood in the field, all wearing the exact same grin.

Then, at the exact same moment, all twenty of them said: "BRILLIANT."

Evy, at the gate, went very quiet. "Oh," she said. "Oh no."

"Oh YES," said twenty Felixes, together.

Alex looked down the row of them — same grin, same *what can I jump off* look, times twenty — and felt a complicated mix of love and mild terror.

"You're all brave," he told them. "And you're all kind. Try to remember the second part."

Twenty Felixes nodded solemnly. Then one of them shouted "CAN WE FLY?" and all twenty tried at once, which answered that question immediately and without dignity.

---

In town, someone else was multiplying too.

His name was Professor Nought, and he was having a considerably worse day than Felix.

Professor Nought had spent his whole career writing books about Nothing — that was his actual subject, capital N, fourteen books of it — and nobody had ever read a single one. Three weeks ago he'd found a strange golden machine in his study that he didn't remember buying, pressed a button on it, and given himself the power to copy himself.

He'd also, entirely by accident, given himself excellent biscuit-making, expert card tricks, and the power to make anyone within ten metres sneeze exactly once. He wasn't sure why that last one had come along. It had, and he had it.

He'd since copied himself seven hundred times, because — as far as he could work out — if there were enough of him standing in one place, somebody would finally have to notice he existed.

He'd heard, from somewhere, that there was a team of children nearby with a whole shelf of magical things.

He wanted that shelf. Not to hurt anyone with it. Just to have something people might ask him about.

When Felix's team reached the town square, seven hundred identical professors stood there in seven hundred identical, slightly-too-large corduroy jackets, all straightening the same wire-rimmed glasses. One was doing a very good card trick to an audience of nobody.

"Hello," said Professor Nought. All seven hundred of him. It was extremely loud.

"Hello," said Alex.

"HELLO," said twenty Felixes, not to be outdone.

Professor Nought looked at the Felixes. Then at Alex. Then at Evy and Isla, who'd caught up by now.

"You're the ones with the shelf," he said.

"Yes," said Alex.

"I'd like the things on it, please."

"No," said all twenty Felixes, together.

---

What happened next was chaotic, and afterward nobody could agree on the exact order of it.

Fourteen Felixes tried to make a wall of themselves by standing on each other's shoulders. It held for four seconds and then didn't.

Evy's compass — the one from her own box, which pointed not north but toward *the real one* — spun in wild circles before settling, very precisely, on a single professor third from the left, whose left shoe was untied.

Isla walked straight up to one of the copies, shook his hand, and asked, "Excuse me — which one are you?"

"I'm not sure any more," he said, quietly.

"I think that might be the problem," said Isla.

Alex noticed something else, watching from the edge of it all: every time Professor Nought used one of his powers — flew a little, went briefly invisible, did a card trick — he looked *sadder* straight afterward, not happier. Like each one was fun for exactly one second and then instantly not enough.

Alex walked over to the professor with the untied shoe and stood very still in front of him. Standing still, saying nothing, was Alex's quietest and most powerful thing.

"Why do you want more powers?" he asked.

Professor Nought opened his mouth. Closed it.

"I wrote fourteen books," he said eventually. "Nobody read them."

"Oh," said Alex.

"I thought if there were enough of me, somebody would finally notice I was here."

Nobody said anything for a moment. Then Felix — the real one, the original — walked over.

"I wanted twenty of me too," Felix said. "This morning. So there'd always be enough Felix to go round."

Professor Nought looked at him properly for the first time.

"Did it work?" he asked.

"No," said Felix. "Turns out it's still just... me. Twenty times. Not any more *me* than usual." He thought about this. "There's already enough of you. One's enough, if it's the right one doing the right thing. You just need better books. Or better friends. Probably both."

Professor Nought blinked hard, several times.

"There's a support group," Felix added. "Thursdays. Janet makes shortbread. Victor's working on a Victoria sponge."

"I do make good biscuits," said Professor Nought, very quietly.

"SEE?" said Felix. "Bring those."

---

Alex blew the whistle once.

All twenty Felixes folded back into one Felix with a sound like a very satisfied *foomp*.

Then Alex blew it twice.

Seven hundred professors rippled and folded and compressed down to a single, slightly rumpled man with an untied shoe and an expression of enormous relief.

"That's better," he said. "I was starting to lose track of which memories were mine."

"Keep the biscuit power," Felix told him. "You've earned that much."

---

That evening, Daddy made tea — one cup each, Felix, Alex, Evy, Isla — and set them down without a word, because the best grown-ups know when the right thing is just to put the tea down and be there.

"Daddy," said Felix, both hands around his cup. "Do you think one of me is enough? Just... one?"

Daddy looked at him for a long moment.

"Felix," he said, "one of you is already more than most people can handle. In a good way."

Felix grinned, enormous and entirely satisfied.

Alex, next to him, didn't say anything at all — but his hand found Felix's under the table, and that said the rest of it.

---

*Professor Nought turned up to the Thursday group the following week with lemon shortbread. Janet declared it "professionally threatening" — the highest compliment she has ever given anyone. He now attends every week, and has started a fifteenth book. This one is about Something.*

*Felix tried to multiply himself again at breakfast the next morning, "just to check." Mummy's expression alone was enough to end the experiment.*

*Alex only ever had to blow the whistle once for the recall. He always only needs once.*

*Amelia, informed of all this well after the fact, wrote a single furious line in her own time about it and left it at that — she'd been out that whole afternoon and was not consulted, a fact she intends to raise at a later date.*
