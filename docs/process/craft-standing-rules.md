# Craft Standing Rules — Felix & Alex Story Universe

*Cross-cutting rules that apply to EVERY story regardless of series or format. These are the hard-won lessons; violating them is what makes a story feel wrong. Series-specific rules live in each series bible; these are universal. Check these before drafting and during every review.*

---

## The characteristic failure mode (watch every pass)

**Don't let the narrator say the quiet part out loud.** Let emotional beats LAND; don't announce them. If a scene already shows two characters are different, don't add a paragraph explaining they're different. If a pause is happening, write the pause — don't narrate "there was a meaningful pause." This tic surfaces roughly once per draft in this universe and cutting it is almost always right. It is *especially* deadly in audio (a listener can't skim past an abstract, imageless narrator-wisdom line).

---

## The Non-Negotiables (universe-wide)

1. **Felix and Alex must both matter.** If either disappears for a page, something is wrong.
2. **The braid: Felix imagines/does; Alex makes it real/holds it.** *(Note: this supersedes the older "Felix does / Alex notices." Alex is not a sensor — he's the doing/finishing engine with his own wants. Both are creative.)*
3. **Adults support, never solve.** Present, anchoring, muscle-when-asked — but the twins fix it. Every time an adult resolves the central problem, the story loses energy.
4. **Felix says the important thing simply.** He cuts to it. That's who he is.
5. **Alex doesn't need many words.** His weight is in what he doesn't say.
6. **Villains (where present) are sad before they are bad.** Always. *(N/A in grounded no-villain series.)*
7. **One physical object at the story's centre.** Something a child can picture and want to hold. Ask it every time: what is the object?
8. **Endings get one laugh and one feeling** (in that order where possible). Don't over-stack the ending — land the plane once.

---

## Want, stakes, and discovery

- **Every story needs a want with a COST**, not just a wish. "They'd like to find X" is soft; "they must do X today or lose the chance / before the storm / or the promise breaks" grips. A soft want was the root of the original Emotion-1.85 failure.
- **Discovery should be EARNED, not frictionless.** Being wrong first, a false lead, a near-miss — makes the real find matter. Look-and-instantly-find is tourism, not adventure.
- **Let the almost-not-working BREATHE** before it resolves. One near-give-up per fix. Don't rush problem→solution in a single movement.
- **Model good emotional regulation where it fits** (notice the feeling, breathe, take a minute, come back) — never preachy. Never use physical-discomfort coping (ice, etc.).

## Age calibration (5-year-old, UK Reception)

**Lands:** concrete sensory detail (not "dark" but "the smell of bark and earth"); sound-effect words (THWIP, WHUMP, SCHLOORP, *tock*); physical comedy; repetition-with-variation; stakes they understand (a lost thing, a sad creature, racing a storm); clear cause-and-effect (max 3 steps); fairness/kindness as values; the sibling dynamic.

**Doesn't land:** irony/sarcasm needing context; backstory in one block (feed it through action); many named characters at once (introduce one at a time, one trait each); abstract concepts without physical manifestation; consequences they can't picture; stories with no physical movement.

**Cast ceiling:** **6 speaking characters maximum** in any one story; 4 active is more comfortable. Guest characters get one clear role and one clear moment.

**Journey rule:** **maximum two obstacle-stops** on any journey. One or two is escalation; three is repetition.

---

## The re-listen layers (build these deliberately)

- **Tier 1** — the emotional payoff, caught on listen 1.
- **Tier 2** — planted objects/lines that only resolve once you know the ending; caught on listen 3–5; makes a child feel *clever* and demand a re-listen. **Seed several per story on purpose.**
- **Tier 3** — deep structural rhymes an adult notices and a child absorbs without naming (e.g. held-then-given).

## Format lengths (read-aloud / narrated)

| Format | Words | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Quick case file | 600–800 | 4–6 min |
| Standard case file | 900–1,200 | 7–9 min |
| Full adventure | 1,500–2,000 | 11–15 min |
| Long special occasion | 2,000–2,500 | 15–18 min |
| Grounded short book (Bramble Wall) | 1,500–2,500 | 8–15 min |

One story is one component of a longer reading session; 15 min max leaves room for chapter books alongside.

---

## Standing content & safety rules

- **Child-appropriate throughout.** Nothing that frightens or winds up at lights-out (edge is allowed *mid*-story if warmth returns fully by the end).
- **Safety modelled truthfully.** If a real device/skill is depicted (radios, safety gear, foraging), get its real behaviour right — never teach a false safety lesson.
- **No adult-authored adventures.** The grown-ups don't secretly set up the quest; the kids author their own.
- **Affirmations woven, never bolted on.** ~2–3 seams per story; they should feel like the story, not a moral tacked on.
